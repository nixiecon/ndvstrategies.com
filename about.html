The story

I had spent my entire career reaching for a version of success I thought people wanted.

And I was good at it. Really good. Until I wasn't.

After a decade in corporate operations, I learned that I'm neurodivergent and had been forcing myself through autistic burnoutDeep, long-term burnout from masking and the daily strain of navigating a world not built for your brain. Unlike regular burnout, a weekend off won't touch it. It can be debilitating for years, sometimes for life, severely affecting well-being and quality of life. for years.

Once I understood how my brain actually works, the rest came into focus. I love work where I build systems and make them run more efficiently. I realized this from what I do to unwind: playing games like SimCity, Surviving Mars, and Planet Coaster, among others. These are basically resource routing and system optimization games. That's not something I learned from work. That's just who I am.

So instead of slogging through corporate politics, I built NDV Strategies so I could help others build systems and make them efficient too.

01

Built for handoff

Every engagement ends with documentation, ownership, and a system that runs without me. You don't get tied to me. You get something that keeps working.

02

Work with what you have, first

My default is making your existing tools work. When something does need to change, I keep the new system as close to the old one as possible. Less learning curve, more adoption.

03

Referral-led

I work primarily with referrals. That keeps it right for both of us. You come in with context; I come in with a high bar to meet.

04

Designed for the hardest case

I build for your worst day, not your best one. The system has to hold up when you're overwhelmed or someone new just inherited it. Build the ramp for whoever needs it most, and it works better for everyone. (It's how I'm wired, too.)
